# Traffic Secrets — audience, temperature, and nurture sequences

Product-agnostic reference for the frameworks in Russell Brunson's *Traffic Secrets* and the Eugene Schwartz awareness levels that underpin them: the 3 Markets, Traffic Temperature, Schwartz's 5 Awareness Levels, the 3 Traffic Types, Dream 100, Soap Opera Sequence, Seinfeld Sequence, and Funnel Hacking.

This file is **theory only**. No concrete services, no tool names, no industry-specific scenarios, no project voice. Substitute the project's own dream customers and proof inventory when applying the frameworks — load them from `docs/services.md` and `docs/brand-voice.md` at the start of every drafting session.

Load this reference when the surface is about *how the reader got to the page* or *what happens after they subscribe*: ads, lead magnets, cold outreach, welcome sequences, nurture emails, and any decision about what tone and awareness level to write for.

## 3 Markets — Health, Wealth, Relationships

Brunson's thesis: every offer that has ever sold at scale lives in one of three primal markets, because these are the only three things humans buy on deep emotional drive.

1. **Health** — the body, energy, weight, mental clarity, longevity, freedom from pain.
2. **Wealth** — money, time (which most B2B buyers interpret as money), status, security, freedom from financial anxiety.
3. **Relationships** — romance, family, community, belonging, recognition.

Every sub-niche is a specific *submarket* inside one of the three. B2B service offers almost always live in **Wealth** — specifically in the "time back", "payroll saved", "anxiety reduced", "control restored" dimensions of Wealth. This matters because the ultimate emotional payoff the copy drives at must match the market: time back, sanity back, control back, one fewer hire to worry about.

**Decision rule:** when drafting a hero, name the submarket explicitly as a test. "The dream customer is in Wealth, specifically time-back-so-the-founder-can-think-strategically-again." If the submarket is vague, the destination in the hook will be vague.

See [ClickFunnels: The Dream 100](https://www.clickfunnels.com/blog/the-dream-100/) for Brunson's own treatment of the 3 Markets in the context of traffic acquisition.

## Traffic Temperature — Cold, Warm, Hot

Borrowed from Eugene Schwartz and adopted by Brunson. Readers arrive at the page at one of three temperatures, and the *right* copy for each is materially different.

1. **Cold traffic** — the reader has never heard of the project, doesn't know the category well, and is only here because an ad or a social post caught their attention. Assume almost no shared vocabulary.
   - **Copy priority:** pain-first. Hook names the specific recurring pain the reader lives with, because that's the only common ground you have.
   - **Story priority:** long. Cold readers need the full Epiphany Bridge with all three False Belief patterns broken. Short copy doesn't convert cold traffic, regardless of hook quality.
   - **Offer priority:** low-commitment. The only viable offer for cold traffic is a lead magnet or a free discovery call. Never a price.

2. **Warm traffic** — the reader has heard of the project, probably from a friend, a newsletter, a referring site, or a retargeting ad. They know roughly what category the offer is in but haven't decided whether it's for them.
   - **Copy priority:** promise-first. Hook names a concrete promise with a number and an objection-reversal.
   - **Story priority:** medium. Warm readers need the Epiphany Bridge short form and at least one of the three False Beliefs broken.
   - **Offer priority:** discovery call or scoped next step. They're ready for a real conversation.

3. **Hot traffic** — the reader already knows the project, the offer, and the price. They're clicking through to the checkout or the booking page.
   - **Copy priority:** product-first. Hook names the offer by name.
   - **Story priority:** short or zero. Hot readers don't need to be re-sold; they need a clean path to action.
   - **Offer priority:** the actual thing, with the actual price, with the simplest possible path to yes.

**Decision rule:** before drafting any surface, identify which temperature the reader will arrive at. A hero at the end of a Dream 100 podcast episode is warm-to-hot; the same hero at the end of a Google Search ad is cold. Same surface, different copy.

## Schwartz Awareness Levels — the 5 stages every reader passes through

Eugene Schwartz's *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966) is the primary source and Brunson references it directly. The five awareness levels are more granular than the three temperatures and are the tool to use when deciding what the opening sentence should say.

1. **Unaware** — the reader doesn't know they have the problem, or doesn't name it as a problem yet.
   - **Opening must:** name the experience the reader is having (not the problem). *"If every Friday afternoon is the same kind of chaos…"*
   - **Never:** name the solution, the category, or the product.

2. **Problem-Aware** — the reader knows they have a problem but doesn't know what solutions exist.
   - **Opening must:** name the problem directly and hint that a solution exists. *"If [specific recurring problem] is costing you [specific cost], there's a category you probably haven't looked at yet."*
   - **Never:** name the product by name. They're not ready.

3. **Solution-Aware** — the reader knows solutions exist, knows the category, but doesn't know the project's specific approach.
   - **Opening must:** differentiate from the category. *"Most [category solutions] do X. This one does Y."*
   - **Never:** pretend the reader hasn't heard of the category. Don't waste lines explaining what they already know.

4. **Product-Aware** — the reader knows the project exists but isn't sure it's for them.
   - **Opening must:** speak to the specific objection that's holding them back. *"If you're wondering whether [project] is a fit for [reader's specific situation]…"*
   - **Never:** over-explain what the project does. They already know.

5. **Most Aware** — the reader knows everything they need to know and is waiting for the right moment or the right push.
   - **Opening must:** give them the push. A specific CTA, a specific constraint ("pilot slots for Q4 close on…"), a specific bonus.
   - **Never:** re-sell. Anything longer than one short paragraph is friction.

**Decision rule:** when drafting the opening of any surface, write down which awareness level the reader is at. Match the opening sentence to the level. Most hero copy fails because it's written for a Solution-Aware reader when the real traffic is Problem-Aware, or vice versa.

For service businesses without pricing published, most cold traffic is Problem-Aware, most referred traffic is Solution-Aware, and very little is Product-Aware or Most Aware. The Problem-Aware opening is usually the safest default for a generic homepage hero.

See [Eugene Schwartz — *Breakthrough Advertising*](https://www.breakthroughadvertisingbook.com/) for the original source; Brunson's treatment in *Traffic Secrets* is derived from Schwartz directly.

## 3 Traffic Types — Earn, Control, Own

Brunson's model for how traffic moves through the funnel infrastructure:

1. **Traffic you don't own** — SEO, organic social, news mentions, word of mouth. Cheap or free, but unpredictable; the platform giveth and taketh.
2. **Traffic you control** — paid ads, sponsorships, affiliate placements. Predictable as long as the project pays, but stops the moment the budget stops.
3. **Traffic you own** — email list, SMS list, subscribed community, push notifications. The only kind of traffic the project owns outright.

**The whole point of a lead magnet** is to convert a type-1 or type-2 visitor into a type-3 owned contact. A lead magnet that doesn't capture contact information is a lead magnet that failed its job, even if the PDF itself is brilliant.

**Decision rule:** for any ToFu bait or lead magnet, the primary success metric is *"did this move a visitor from not-owned to owned?"*, not *"did the visitor find the content useful?"* — the second is necessary but not sufficient.

## Dream 100 — finding the 100 places the dream customer already is

Brunson's strategy for audience acquisition: the project's dream customers already gather in ~100 specific places — podcasts, newsletters, forums, conferences, YouTube channels, Slack/Discord communities, LinkedIn groups, industry publications. Find those 100 places, and either *work your way in* (create content for them, guest-appear, contribute) or *buy your way in* (sponsor, advertise, pay for placement). Both are valid; the balance depends on the project's budget and the industry.

The 6-step Dream 100 process (condensed):

1. **List the 100** — be specific. "Podcasts about [specific subdiscipline]", not "marketing podcasts". The list should be discoverable in a single weekend by someone who knows the industry.
2. **Segment the list** by platform type (podcast, newsletter, community, publication, etc.). Different platforms need different outreach.
3. **Identify the gatekeeper** for each — the host, the editor, the moderator, the media buyer. The project will talk to these specific people, not to "the podcast".
4. **Work your way in first.** Create content, leave comments, answer questions, show up. This is the *earned* side of the strategy.
5. **Buy your way in second.** Once there's a relationship, sponsorships and paid placements convert much better than cold ad buys.
6. **Iterate** — the Dream 100 list refreshes every 3–6 months. Places die, new places emerge, the project's positioning changes.

**Decision rule:** the Dream 100 is out of scope for most landing-page drafting sessions, but the *moment* the project needs to decide where traffic will come from, load this section. Until then, the Dream 100 is a future strategic document, not a copy decision.

See [ClickFunnels: The Dream 100](https://www.clickfunnels.com/blog/the-dream-100/) for the full 6-step treatment.

## Soap Opera Sequence — the 5-email welcome

Every new subscriber — whether from a lead magnet, a newsletter signup, or a discovery-call booking — gets a 5-email welcome sequence that follows a soap-opera structure. The point is to build the relationship with the Attractive Character before pitching anything.

The 5 emails (one per day is the canonical cadence; the project can stretch to one per 2–3 days for B2B):

1. **Email 1 — Set the stage.** Introduce the narrator. Tell the reader what to expect from the next four emails. Create curiosity for email 2.
2. **Email 2 — Backstory and high drama.** The Attractive Character's origin story. Where the narrator came from, the formative struggle. End on a cliffhanger — the narrator is about to hit the Wall but email 2 stops just before.
3. **Email 3 — The Wall.** The lowest point. What almost broke the narrator. The reader has to believe the narrator nearly didn't make it. End on the moment the Epiphany is about to happen.
4. **Email 4 — The Epiphany and the hidden benefit.** The realisation that changed everything + the specific advantage the reader gets from the narrator's approach that they wouldn't get elsewhere. This is the first email with a soft CTA.
5. **Email 5 — Urgency and the main CTA.** The hard ask. Specific, scoped, time-bound or capacity-bound if possible. The Risk Reversal spelled out explicitly.

**Rules:**

- Each email is 150–400 words. Longer is worse.
- Subject lines are curiosity-driven, not feature-driven. *"What I almost did instead"* > *"5 reasons to book a call"*.
- The sequence is the same 5 emails regardless of how the subscriber arrived; only the CTA in email 5 changes.
- The sequence runs *once*. After it ends, the subscriber moves to the [Seinfeld Sequence](#seinfeld-sequence--daily-nurture-after-the-welcome).

See [Soap Opera Sequence full breakdown with templates](https://sequenzy.com/blog/soap-opera-email-sequence) for a worked-example format.

## Seinfeld Sequence — daily nurture after the welcome

Named after the sitcom, the Seinfeld Sequence is the "about nothing" email strategy that keeps a subscriber warm indefinitely after the Soap Opera ends.

The pattern:

- **Cadence:** one email per day (or every 2–3 days for B2B), indefinitely.
- **Length:** short — 100–300 words.
- **Structure:** a tiny everyday story → a loose-but-real connection to the project's work → a soft CTA at the bottom.
- **Story sources:** the narrator's actual life (anchored in the Attractive Character's archetype). Something that happened at the supermarket, in a meeting, while reading a book, while watching a film. The hook is *specificity*, not relevance.
- **CTA:** always the same or one of 2–3 recurring ones. The point is not to sell aggressively; the point is to stay present in the subscriber's inbox so that when they're ready, the project is the obvious choice.

**Rules:**

- The story must actually be true. Faked Seinfeld emails are transparent and damage the Attractive Character.
- The connection to the project can be loose. Brunson's rule: *"any story can connect to any offer if you write the bridge sentence well."*
- Email fatigue is real — if open rates drop below the project's baseline, back off the cadence, don't crank it up.
- The sequence is *the point*. Most subscribers convert during the Seinfeld phase, not during the Soap Opera phase.

## Funnel Hacking — reverse-engineering competitors

Brunson's mental model behind most of his recommendations. Funnel Hacking is the systematic practice of signing up for competitors' funnels, tracking every email they send, every page they ladder through, every upsell, every downsell — and extracting the patterns.

**What to track when funnel-hacking a competitor:**

1. The hook on the landing page.
2. The opt-in bait and what it delivers.
3. The thank-you page and the immediate upsell (if any).
4. Every email in the sequence, in order, with subject lines and body structure.
5. The cadence of the emails (hours between, days between).
6. The pricing structure, where it's revealed, and how many upsells/downsells follow.
7. The tone shifts across the sequence.
8. The CTAs — how many, how worded, how reversed.

**Rules:**

- Funnel-hack 3–5 competitors at a time, not just one. Patterns only emerge when multiple funnels are compared.
- Funnel-hack the *structure*, not the copy. Copying copy is plagiarism and does not work because it's detached from the other project's Attractive Character. Copying structure is legitimate research.
- Funnel-hack across industries. The best patterns for a B2B service often come from the consumer side (fitness, finance, relationships), not from direct competitors.

Funnel Hacking is out of scope for a single drafting session but is the right answer when the project is stuck and can't figure out which framework to use on a given surface: go find five projects whose audience overlaps and see what they're doing.
