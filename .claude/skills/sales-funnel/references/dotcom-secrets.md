# DotCom Secrets — landing-page mechanics

Product-agnostic reference for the landing-page frameworks from Russell Brunson's *DotCom Secrets*: the Secret Formula, Hook-Story-Offer, the Epiphany Bridge, the Value Ladder, and the Application Funnel.

This file is **theory only**. No concrete services, no tool names, no industry-specific scenarios, no project voice. Substitute the project's own dream customers, services, vocabulary, and proof inventory when applying the frameworks — load them from `docs/services.md` and `docs/brand-voice.md` at the start of every drafting session. If a worked example would be useful for a specific project, write it natively from those docs; do not borrow examples from this file.

## The Secret Formula — designing the page around the reader

Brunson's [Secret Formula](https://www.clickfunnels.com/blog/the-secret-formula-for-building-an-online-business/) is a four-question filter to run before writing a single sentence. Answer all four before drafting.

1. **Who is the dream customer?** — the specific person the page is for, not a category. Pick one archetype from `docs/services.md`. The bar: a single recognisable person doing a specific recurring task at a specific moment of the week. *"Small businesses"*, *"developers"*, *"founders"* are categories, not archetypes. Reject any answer at category level.

2. **Where are they?** — physically, online, and emotionally. Physically: the specific market the project serves. Online: the platforms this archetype actually uses, which are usually narrower and messier than a marketing deck would suggest. Emotionally: where the reader is when they encounter the page — almost always tired, almost always behind on something.

3. **What bait brings them in?** — the lead magnet or entry point. Good baits are specific, narrowly scoped, and promise one concrete win the reader can act on in one read. Bad baits are vague (*"a free consultation"*). Load the project's actual baits from `docs/services.md` if listed.

4. **What destination are they after?** — the transformation, not the feature. Destinations are always emotional reclamations: time back, sleep back, control back, sanity back, a normal evening, one fewer person to hire. Never the literal feature.

**Decision rule:** if the answer to any of the four is vague, stop writing and ask the developer a targeted question. Copy written without a clear dream customer always sounds like a consultancy deck.

## Dan Kennedy — Message-Market-Media Triangle

Before applying Hook-Story-Offer to any surface, pass the draft through Dan Kennedy's Results Triangle from *No B.S. Direct Marketing* (Ch. 10 "The Results Triangle"). Kennedy's thesis is that the three inputs are interdependent and one weak vertex collapses the other two, regardless of how good the copy is on its own:

> **"Every business, past or present, requires these three things to prosper: a Marketing Message, a Media to deliver it, and a Market to receive and respond to it... It is a closed triangle."**
> — Dan Kennedy, *No B.S. Direct Marketing*, Ch. 10

The three vertices:

1. **Market** — *who*. The specific dream customer archetype from `docs/services.md`. Market governs the other two: the Message is written *for this market*, and the Media is the channel *this market actually uses*. Wrong market is unrecoverable — no copy fixes it.
2. **Message** — *what*. The specific offer, framed in the reader's own language, written backwards from the reader's pain. Kennedy on the direction of drafting:
   > **"Most great sales copy is written backwards, from the customer's interests, desires, frustrations, fears, thoughts, feeling, and experiences..."**
   > — *No B.S. Direct Marketing*, Ch. 6
3. **Media** — *where*. The channel the dream customer is actually reading / listening to / opening. Kennedy's rule: the right Media is discovered by following the Market, never by following Media fashion.

Kennedy's load-bearing point for every drafting session is the Message-to-Market match:

> **"Each person believes himself, his business, his situation, his needs to be unique... I call this The Secret of Message-to-Market Match."**
> — *No B.S. Direct Marketing*, Ch. 11

**How to apply alongside Brunson's Secret Formula:**

- Kennedy's **Market** maps directly to Brunson's *"Who is the dream customer?"* — write down one archetype from `docs/services.md` and do not draft until it is concrete.
- Kennedy's **Message** maps onto Hook-Story-Offer but demands the direction-check: is this written *backwards from the customer's pain*, or forwards from the project's features? Forwards drafts fail.
- Kennedy's **Media** maps onto the *Where are they?* vertex of the Secret Formula and onto the 3 Traffic Types / Dream 100 in [`traffic-secrets.md`](traffic-secrets.md).

**Decision rule:** before drafting any surface, state the triangle in three words (*Market: X, Message: Y, Media: Z*). If any vertex is "everyone / anyone / general", stop and resolve it. The most common failure is a sharp Message written for a vague Market — the copy reads fine in isolation and converts nothing.

Kennedy's own sites paywall most of the primary text. The cleanest available secondary sources are [With Se7en — *No B.S. Direct Marketing* chapter breakdown](https://www.withse7en.com/marketing/no-b-s-direct-marketing-by-dan-kennedy) and [Bookey — *Magnetic Marketing* summary](https://www.bookey.app/book/magnetic-marketing); the Kennedy FAQ PDF is also useful: [HVC Kennedy FAQ](https://cdn2.hubspot.net/hubfs/4110650/HVC-FAQS.pdf).

## Gary Halbert — A-pile/B-pile and the Starving Crowd

Two agnostic gut-checks from Gary Halbert's newsletters that predate Brunson by 20 years and still govern how direct-response copy opens and which markets it's worth opening for.

### A-pile / B-pile — the pre-hook test

From Halbert's September 1, 1986 letter *"The Amazing Direct Mail Secret Of A Desperate Nerd From Ohio"*:

> **"Everybody in the world divides his mail into two piles which I call the A-Pile and the B-Pile. The A-Pile contains letters that are, (or appear to be), personal. The B-Pile contains everything else..."**
> — [The Gary Halbert Letter — *Nerd*](https://www.thegaryhalbertletter.com/nerd/)

The test translates directly to landing pages, emails, and ads. Every hook either reads *personal* (A-pile — one specific person writing to one specific reader about one specific situation) or *corporate* (B-pile — a brand talking at a market). B-pile copy is filtered out before it's read. A-pile copy earns the next sentence.

**How to apply:** before accepting a draft hook, ask — *if this were a physical letter on my desk, would I open it, or would I throw it out with the flyers?* If the answer isn't an obvious "open", the hook is B-pile and needs a rewrite. Hooks that start with *"We are…"*, *"Our team…"*, *"Empowering businesses…"* are always B-pile.

### Starving Crowd — the pre-market test

From Halbert's newsletter *Direct Marketing To A Starving Crowd*:

> **"If you and I both owned a hamburger stand and we were in a contest to see who could sell the most hamburgers, what advantages would you most like to have on your side?... The only advantage I want... Is... A Starving Crowd!"**
> — [The Gary Halbert Letter — *Starving Crowd*](https://www.thegaryhalbertletter.com/newsletters/direct_marketing_to_a_starving_crowd.htm)

Halbert's point is that the market itself is the largest lever in any marketing decision. A mediocre offer sold to a starving crowd outperforms a world-class offer sold to a full one, every time. This is the sharper sibling of Brunson's [Dream Customer](#the-secret-formula--designing-the-page-around-the-reader) question in the Secret Formula.

**How to apply:** before drafting, name the specific "hunger" the project's dream customer is living with *right now*. If the hunger is speculative (*"they might want…"*), the crowd isn't starving and the copy has no work to do. The Secret Formula's dream-customer question and the [Schwartz awareness levels](traffic-secrets.md#schwartz-awareness-levels--the-5-stages-every-reader-passes-through) in [`traffic-secrets.md`](traffic-secrets.md) are the tools to resolve this, but Halbert's hamburger test is the fastest sanity-check.

### Misattribution note — the quarter-inch drill

The "people don't want a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter-inch hole" line is commonly misattributed to Halbert. It is **not** his. The idea is Theodore Levitt's (*Marketing Myopia*, HBR 1960), and the exact phrasing was popularised by Leo McGivena in 1942: *"Last year one million quarter-inch drills were sold — not because people wanted quarter-inch drills but because they wanted quarter-inch holes."* Use the quote if it's useful, but attribute it correctly. Full provenance: [The Tom Roach — *Modern Marketing Myopia*](https://thetomroach.com/2021/05/01/modern-marketing-myopia-is-marketing-losing-perspective/).

## Hook-Story-Offer — the shape of every page

Every page, section, ad, and email has three parts. If any one is off, the page does not convert.

### Hook — stop the scroll

The hook is the first headline and subheadline a reader sees. Its only job is to make them stay for one more line. Three hook patterns that reliably work for service businesses:

1. **Name-the-pain + different-solution** — call out the exact recurring task the reader does today, then hint that the fix is not the obvious one. The specificity of the pain is what stops the scroll; the misdirection of the fix is what earns the next line.

2. **Concrete promise with a number** — bind the promise to a specific magnitude and a specific objection-reversal (*"…without changing your software"*, *"…in the first month"*, *"…without hiring"*). Numbers do the work; the *"without X"* clause defuses the most common objection.

3. **Anti-stance / counter-position** — name what the project is *not*, in the language the reader uses to describe the alternative they distrust. Use the project's signature metaphor here if it has one — but only if `docs/brand-voice.md` allows it on this surface.

Hooks to reject on sight:

- Anything starting with "We are…" or "Our…"
- "Empowering businesses to…"
- "Your trusted partner in…"
- "Digital transformation" in any form
- Anything that could describe every company on the planet

The hook is also where the reader's awareness level matters. Cold traffic needs a pain-first hook; warm traffic needs a promise hook; hot traffic needs a product-name hook. See [`traffic-secrets.md`](traffic-secrets.md) → Awareness levels before picking a hook pattern.

### Story — Epiphany Bridge

Brunson: people buy emotionally and justify logically. The Epiphany Bridge is the emotional scaffolding. Short form is 5 beats, one short paragraph each:

1. **Backstory** — the reader's "before world", recognisable in two sentences. Use the reader's actual recurring pain as named in `docs/services.md`, not abstractions. Concrete nouns only.

2. **The Wall** — what stopped them working. The workaround that keeps failing. The thing they tried that almost worked but didn't. This is where the stakes land: hours, errors, weekends, missed milestones, a specific personal cost. The cost must feel personal, not aggregate.

3. **Epiphany** — the realisation that unlocks everything, framed as a discovery the reader could have made themselves. Shape: *"It turned out the answer was not [obvious thing], it was [smaller, surprising thing]."* The epiphany must reframe the *problem*, not merely improve the answer — see [`expert-secrets.md`](expert-secrets.md) → New Opportunity vs Improvement Offer for why this distinction is load-bearing.

4. **Framework** — the solution described as the *outcome the reader experiences*, never as components, tools, or jargon. One sentence. Never name internal tools, vendors, or models — the project's tech-stack ban list lives in `docs/brand-voice.md`. This beat is optional for simple consumer offers; required for service copy where the reader can't picture what they'd buy from the Epiphany alone.

5. **Transformation + proof** — what life looks like on the other side, anchored by something concrete. Pull real proof from `docs/services.md` → proof inventory. If real proof does not exist yet, say so honestly and reframe the absence as a feature of the current phase.

Brunson's long form in *Expert Secrets* expands this into up to 10 beats (Backstory → Desire → External Struggle → Internal Struggle → Wall → Epiphany → Plan → Conflict → Achievement → Transformation). Use the long form for case-study pages and webinars; the short 5-beat form everywhere else.

The story also has to break the reader's [False Beliefs](expert-secrets.md#false-belief-patterns--vehicle-internal-external) — Vehicle (does this method work?), Internal (can I do it?), External (will the world let me?). If the story only addresses one belief, the other two stop the sale later. See [`expert-secrets.md`](expert-secrets.md) → False Belief Patterns.

### Offer — the close

The offer is whatever the next rung of the project's Value Ladder is. For projects in the pilot-discovery / pre-pricing phase, the offer is almost always a **discovery call**. Never a checkout. Never a price. Never a "buy now".

The CTA block must contain, in this order:

1. **CTA headline** — frames the call as valuable, not salesy. The Brunson move: a mutual fit check — *"tell us about you, we'll tell you honestly if we can help"* — not a pitch.
2. **Three bullets of what the reader gets from the call** — a diagnosis, a yes/no, a rough scope. Not a sales pitch.
3. **Risk reversal, explicit** — free, exact duration, no commitment, no slide deck, no follow-up email sequence. Spell out every specific fear the reader has. Generic *"no commitment"* is not enough.
4. **Mild scarcity, only if it is true** — real constraints stated as facts. Never fake urgency. Countdown timers are banned.
5. **One primary CTA button** — one or two words. No competing buttons above the fold.

For offers where the price *is* published, construct the offer with the [Stack Slide](expert-secrets.md#stack-slide--the-offer-layered-and-re-stated) and [Hormozi Value Equation](expert-secrets.md#hormozi-value-equation--the-math-of-an-irresistible-offer) from [`expert-secrets.md`](expert-secrets.md).

**Decision rule:** if the page has more than one primary CTA above the fold, delete one. Two CTAs is the most common failure mode in Brunson's own audits.

## Value Ladder — the long game, and its collapsed form

Brunson's [Value Ladder](https://www.clickfunnels.com/blog/value-ladder/) is the five-rung shape of a fully productised business: **free → entry → core → high-ticket → continuity**.

For projects in the pilot-discovery phase (no public pricing, scope decided after the call), the ladder is **collapsed** — most of the rungs exist conceptually but are not transactable until after a conversation. Map it to a lead funnel instead:

| Ladder rung | Pre-pricing equivalent                                       |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Bait / free | Before/after example, industry-specific PDF, free audit call |
| Entry       | 20–30 min discovery call                                     |
| Core        | Scoped pilot build (priced after the call)                   |
| High-ticket | Multiple integrations, full engagement                       |
| Continuity  | Maintenance / managed service (a fixed monthly fee)          |

Every page should make the next-rung action obvious, and in the pre-pricing phase that next rung is almost always the discovery call. ToFu bait exists to lead to the discovery call, not to sell itself.

**Decision rule:** when in doubt about which rung to push on a page, push on the discovery call. That is the current bottleneck in the funnel for any project that has not published prices yet.

Once the project publishes pricing, the ladder uncollapses. The discovery-call-as-offer pattern gives way to a real product-as-offer pattern, and this section should be revisited at that point.

## Application Funnel — no-published-pricing B2B

Brunson recommends the Application Funnel for higher-trust services priced over $2k / €2k, or for anything where the price depends on scope. The reader does not buy on the page — they apply for a slot, and the call qualifies fit.

The pattern:

1. The page pre-sells with hook / story / case-study style content.
2. The CTA is "apply" or "book a call", not "buy".
3. The form or booking flow has just enough friction to qualify — a few questions about the business and the pain. Not a 30-field interrogation.
4. The call itself is positioned as a mutual fit check: *tell us about you, we'll tell you honestly if we can help*.
5. Scarcity is real and stated: a limited number of pilot slots, not a fake countdown.

Any project without published pricing — because pricing depends on scope, because the project is still in discovery, or because the customer is high-trust B2B — should use the Application Funnel by default for every bottom-of-funnel page.

## Common failure modes

- **Feature dumps** — listing what the product does instead of what the reader gets. Fix by running each sentence through *"so what?"* until it lands on a destination.
- **Two CTAs above the fold** — kills conversion. Delete one.
- **Consultant voice creeping in** — *"We partner with businesses to…"*. Rewrite as direct-action verbs the reader would use themselves.
- **Named tech stack** — vendor names, model names, internal tooling, AI/engineering jargon. Replace with reader-facing descriptions. The project's full ban list lives in `docs/brand-voice.md` → tech stack ban.
- **Fake proof** — invented client names, fake numbers, fake testimonials. If proof does not exist, position the honesty as a feature.
- **Missing risk reversal** — never assume the reader will trust a 20-minute call is free. Say it.
- **Hero that is a paragraph** — the hero is two lines. Pack the rest into the story section.
- **Improvement-offer hook** — "do X better/faster/cheaper" converts poorly. If the hook reads as an improvement, rewrite as a [New Opportunity](expert-secrets.md#new-opportunity-vs-improvement-offer) per [`expert-secrets.md`](expert-secrets.md).
