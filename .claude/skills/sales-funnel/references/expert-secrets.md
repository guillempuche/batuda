# Expert Secrets — mass movement and offer construction

Product-agnostic reference for the frameworks in Russell Brunson's *Expert Secrets*: the Attractive Character, the Cause, the New Opportunity, the Big Domino, False Belief Patterns, the Stack Slide, the Hormozi Value Equation, and the Perfect Webinar.

This file is **theory only**. No concrete services, no tool names, no industry-specific scenarios, no project voice. Substitute the project's own dream customers, services, and proof inventory when applying the frameworks — load them from `docs/services.md` and `docs/brand-voice.md` at the start of every drafting session.

Load this reference when the surface is about *who the project is* and *what the offer is*: about pages, manifesto pages, pricing pages, webinar scripts, long-form sales letters, and anywhere the reader needs to trust the narrator before they trust the offer.

## Attractive Character — the narrator the reader follows

Every piece of copy has a narrator. Readers don't follow companies, they follow *characters they relate to*. The Attractive Character is the consistent voice behind every page, email, and post — the "I" (or "we") the reader grows attached to over time.

The Attractive Character has **four elements**, **four archetypes**, and **six storylines** it uses over and over.

### Four elements (every Attractive Character has all four)

1. **Backstory** — where the narrator came from, the formative struggle. Not a CV. Not "founded in 2019". The specific origin story that makes the rest of the narrator's positions make sense. Two or three sentences maximum per use.
2. **Parables** — short third-person stories the narrator tells to illustrate a point without pitching. Parables are how the narrator teaches without lecturing. Every parable must have a payoff sentence.
3. **Character flaws** — the narrator's specific, admitted weaknesses. Brunson's rule: perfection is unrelatable, so hand-pick 1–2 flaws that are true and that humanise the narrator. Flaws should never undermine competence in the core service; they should make the narrator feel like a real person.
4. **Polarity** — what the narrator is *for* and what they are *against*. Polarity is the filter that draws some readers closer and repels others. A narrator without polarity has no fans. Never neutralise the project's positions in pursuit of broader appeal — neutralisation is the fastest way to become invisible.

### Four archetypes (pick one, stay consistent)

Brunson's four types. The project's Attractive Character usually fits one of these; if it fits two, the stronger one is primary and the other appears in specific storylines.

1. **The Leader** — has been in the reader's shoes, has built the thing, teaches others how to do it. Speaks from completed experience. Voice is matter-of-fact and proof-heavy.
2. **The Adventurer / Crusader** — on a mission the reader is invited to join. Voice is urgent, mission-driven, polarising. Good fit when the project has a clear Cause (see next section).
3. **The Reporter / Evangelist** — has interviewed or studied the people who've built the thing, and shares what they've found. Voice is curious, research-driven. Good fit when the narrator is newer and doesn't have proof of their own yet — honesty about being a reporter is better than faking being a leader.
4. **The Reluctant Hero** — didn't want the role, fell into it, stayed because the work matters. Voice is understated, modest, anti-guru. Good fit for technical founders who are uncomfortable with marketing-speak.

### Six storylines (use these, reuse them, rotate them)

Every story the narrator tells fits one of six shapes. Rotating across them keeps the content fresh without inventing new material.

1. **Loss & Redemption** — lost everything, rebuilt, here's what was learned.
2. **Us vs Them** — a shared enemy (an approach, an industry habit, a misconception). Polarising by design.
3. **Before & After** — concrete transformation, anchored in specifics.
4. **Amazing Discovery** — the narrator stumbled onto something that wasn't supposed to work but did.
5. **Secret Telling** — the narrator shares something most people in the field keep quiet about.
6. **Third-Person Testimonial** — the narrator tells someone else's story (customer, student, peer) to prove the approach.

**Decision rule:** before drafting any piece of copy, identify *which* archetype the narrator is and *which* storyline the story beat fits. If neither is clear, the voice will drift mid-page.

See [ClickFunnels: Building the Attractive Character](https://www.clickfunnels.com/blog/attractive-character/) for Brunson's full treatment.

## Cause / Mass Movement — why readers join, not buy

Brunson's Expert Secrets thesis: people don't buy products, they join movements. A "mass movement" is a coherent belief system the reader can opt into that makes the product the obvious next step. Three ingredients:

1. **A charismatic leader** — the Attractive Character above.
2. **A future-based cause** — a specific vision of a better world the reader already wants to live in.
3. **A new opportunity** — the vehicle that gets them there (see next section).

Most service copy skips this layer. That's fine for commodity purchases where the reader already believes the problem is real and the category is valid. It's *not* fine when the reader has to be convinced that the project's entire way of thinking is worth taking seriously — which is usually true for new categories, high-trust B2B, and any project that believes its approach is materially different from the default.

**How to apply:** before writing the manifesto / about / homepage, answer two questions in one sentence each:

- *What is the future world the project is working towards?* (Specific. Not "a better world". Not "digital transformation".)
- *What belief about the current world must the reader accept for the project's approach to make sense?*

Those two sentences become the scaffolding for the Big Domino (next).

## New Opportunity vs Improvement Offer

**This is probably the single most load-bearing concept in the entire sales-funnel skill.** Most copy fails at the hook level because it tries to sell an *improvement* when it should be selling a *new opportunity*.

- **Improvement offer** = "Do the thing you're already doing, but better / faster / cheaper / smarter." Reader's instinctive response: *I already tried, it's hard, I'm tired, maybe next quarter.* Conversion is low because the reader is measuring the offer against the friction of changing a process they already believe is the right shape.
- **New Opportunity offer** = "There's a different kind of thing entirely. Not a better X. A thing X readers didn't know existed." Reader's instinctive response: *wait, what?* — and they read the next line. Conversion is higher because the reader has no prior friction to compare against; the offer lives in a category they haven't yet tired of.

Brunson's [direct writing on this](https://marketingsecrets.com/blog/ditch-your-improvement-offer) is explicit: he refuses to sell improvement offers because they convert poorly, regardless of how good the improvement is.

**How to apply:**

1. Write the hook as you'd naturally draft it.
2. Ask: *does this read as "do X better" or "do a different kind of X"?*
3. If improvement: rewrite the hook so the *thing itself* is reframed. The reframe almost always lives in *which problem* the offer solves, not *how well* it solves the old problem. Example reframe patterns:
   - "Not a faster [X], but what happens when you stop needing [X] at all."
   - "Not [X] 2.0, but the thing that makes [X] unnecessary."
   - "The [category the reader knows] built for the [specific context the reader lives in]."
4. The Story beat must then match: the Epiphany has to reframe the *problem*, not merely introduce a better *solution*. If the Epiphany is "I found a better tool", the offer is an improvement. If it's "I realised the whole frame was wrong", the offer is a new opportunity.

**Decision rule for the validation checklist:** every hook-story-offer draft is checked for improvement-offer leakage. If the draft passes the "reader already knows this is possible" test, it's probably an improvement offer and needs a rewrite.

## Big Domino Statement — the one sentence that makes everything else irrelevant

Brunson's *Big Domino* thesis: every page should ladder up to one single belief the reader has to accept. If they accept it, everything else in the offer becomes obvious. If they don't, nothing else in the offer can save the sale.

The template:

> **"If I can get them to believe that [X] is the key to [their desired outcome], AND that it's only attainable through [my specific new opportunity], then all their other objections and concerns become irrelevant, and they have to buy."**

The page exists to knock over the Big Domino. Every section of the page is either (a) naming the Domino, (b) proving the Domino, or (c) showing what life looks like once the Domino is accepted.

**How to apply:**

1. Write the Big Domino sentence first, before any copy. It's the thesis of the page.
2. For every paragraph on the page, ask: *is this paragraph helping the reader accept the Big Domino?* If not, cut it.
3. The hook hints at the Big Domino. The story proves it. The offer is the obvious consequence of accepting it.

**Example shape (agnostic):** *"If I can get the reader to believe that [the bottleneck is actually their intake process, not their team size], and that [this is only solvable by routing work before it reaches a human, not by hiring more humans], then every other objection becomes irrelevant."* — that's a Big Domino. Everything on the page is either naming the intake bottleneck or proving the route-before-human pattern.

**Decision rule:** if you can't state the Big Domino in one sentence, the page has no spine. Stop drafting and write the Big Domino first.

## False Belief Patterns — Vehicle, Internal, External

Every prospect has beliefs that stop them from buying. Brunson groups them into three patterns. The story must break *all three*, in order, or the offer collapses at the weakest belief.

1. **Vehicle belief** — *"Does this method actually work?"*
   - Reader's skepticism: the approach itself is unproven, they've seen things like this fail, the category is noise.
   - Break it with: proof, mechanism, third-party evidence, a concrete explanation of *why* the vehicle works that the reader can verify.

2. **Internal belief** — *"Can I actually do it / get it / use it?"*
   - Reader's skepticism: even if the method works in general, it won't work *for them* — wrong company size, wrong team, wrong industry, not technical enough, too busy.
   - Break it with: a recognisable analogue customer, a before-and-after that matches the reader's starting conditions, a framework the reader can picture themselves operating.

3. **External belief** — *"Will the world let me?"*
   - Reader's skepticism: even if the method works and they could do it, their environment won't permit it — their boss won't approve, their industry won't accept it, compliance will kill it, they won't have time, their clients won't buy into it.
   - Break it with: risk reversal, a low-commitment first step, permission-shaping ("the regulator already allows…"), or a named-alternative outcome ("worst case, you walk away with a free audit").

**How to apply:**

1. Before drafting the story, write each false belief out as a literal sentence the reader would think. Don't skip this; the beliefs you *assume* are wrong are the ones the copy will miss.
2. Order them Vehicle → Internal → External. The order matters: if the reader doesn't believe the *method* works, no amount of personal reassurance helps.
3. Every story beat maps to breaking one belief. If a story beat doesn't break any of the three, cut it.

**Decision rule for the validation checklist:** "False Belief test" — can you point at which specific sentence breaks each of the three beliefs? If you can't, the story has a gap.

## Hero's Two Journeys — external and internal

Brunson's adaptation of the classic hero's journey: every transformation has two parallel arcs, and a story that only tracks one of them doesn't land.

- **External journey** — the visible outcome. Hours back, revenue up, error rate down, weekends reclaimed. This is what the Transformation beat in the Epiphany Bridge tracks by default.
- **Internal journey** — the identity shift. *Who the narrator became* through the external journey. Went from "the founder who was always behind on ops" to "the founder who trusts the system". From "the team that was constantly in triage" to "the team that has opinions about strategy again". This is what most service copy skips.

**How to apply:** in the Transformation beat of the Epiphany Bridge, include both arcs in consecutive sentences. External first (concrete, measurable), then internal (identity, how the reader sees themselves now).

For the speaker-register question the skill asks (*solo workshop I vs team we*), the internal journey is the payoff the register must match. A solo-workshop *I* narrator's internal journey is almost always about craft and autonomy; a team *we* narrator's internal journey is almost always about scale and shared mission. Match the journey to the register before drafting.

## Stack Slide — the offer, layered and re-stated

Used on pricing pages, webinar closes, and long-form sales letters. The Stack is the moment where the full offer is spelled out, component by component, each with its own value.

The structure:

1. **List every component of the offer as a discrete item**, in order. Each item is one line: *what it is + what it does for the reader + individual value*.
2. **Sum the individual values** (real values, not inflated ones — fake values kill trust).
3. **Re-state the total** ("Total value: $X"). Pause.
4. **Introduce the actual price.** Always lower than the stacked total, usually by a factor of 3–10x.
5. **Re-stack** — repeat the list one more time, fast, as a reminder.
6. **Risk reversal + CTA** — the same risk-reversal pattern from Hook-Story-Offer.

**Rule:** the Stack only works if every component is something the reader actually gets and wants. Padding the Stack with meaningless bonuses ("a welcome email!", "access to our newsletter!") degrades the entire page. Each component must pass the "would you pay for this as a standalone thing?" test.

The Stack is skippable for the pre-pricing / Application Funnel phase, because there is no price to frame. Revisit when the project publishes prices.

## Hormozi Value Equation — the math of an irresistible offer

From Alex Hormozi's *$100M Offers*, widely adopted by the Brunson crowd as the construction framework for offers. The equation:

> **Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)**

Four levers. A better offer pulls *any* lever meaningfully, not all four equally.

1. **Dream Outcome** — the reader's specific emotional payoff (not the feature). Maximise with precision: a vague outcome is worth less than a sharp one, even if the sharp one is smaller in magnitude.
2. **Perceived Likelihood of Achievement** — the reader's confidence that *for them*, the outcome will actually happen. Maximise with proof, guarantees, risk reversal, recognisable case studies, and "done-for-you" framing.
3. **Time Delay** — how long the reader has to wait before they feel the outcome. Minimise by delivering a partial win in the first week or the first session. Any offer that says "in 6 months you'll see results" should be rewritten to "within 7 days you'll see [smaller, earlier win]".
4. **Effort & Sacrifice** — how much the reader has to *do* to get the outcome. Minimise with done-for-you components, pre-built templates, automated setup, and clearly scoped time commitments.

**How to apply when constructing an offer:** score the current draft on each lever (1–5). The lever with the lowest score is the one to rewrite first. Most service offers fail on *Time Delay* (the first win is too far out) and *Perceived Likelihood* (not enough proof or reassurance).

**Decision rule:** an irresistible offer has the numerator as high as possible *and* the denominator as low as possible. If both halves aren't being pulled on, the offer is sub-optimal regardless of how good the underlying service is.

## Cialdini — 7 Principles of Influence

Robert Cialdini's *Influence* (1984, revised 2021) is the source behind most of the closes and micro-commitments that Brunson, Hormozi, and the direct-response tradition layer on top of Hook-Story-Offer. The original 6 principles are now **7**: Unity was added in the 2021 revision.

1. **Reciprocity** — people feel obliged to return favours. Give before you ask. Free audits, lead magnets, actually-useful emails *before* the CTA do this work.
2. **Commitment & Consistency** — people align future actions with past public statements. Trial closes work because each small yes makes the big yes feel like the consistent next move.
3. **Social Proof** — people copy what similar others are doing. "Similar" is the key word: generic logos convert worse than one named customer the reader identifies with.
4. **Authority** — people defer to credentials, expertise, and authority symbols. Years of experience, published work, named engagements. Cialdini's rule: demonstrate expertise *before* asking for the yes, not after.
5. **Liking** — people say yes to people they like. Liking compounds on similarity, compliments, and cooperation towards a shared goal. This is why the Attractive Character's flaws matter.
6. **Scarcity** — people value what's rare or disappearing. Only real scarcity — invented scarcity damages every other principle at once.
7. **Unity** — the "we" factor. Shared identity (*"we're the kind of people who…"*) rather than shared category (*"we're both in SaaS"*). Added in the 2021 revision because it was under-treated in the original.

Cialdini's own framing of why these seven work across almost every setting:

> **"These principles represent psychological universals of influence — they work not because of what we're selling, but because of how human beings are built to decide."**
> — [influenceatwork.com — 7 Principles of Persuasion](https://www.influenceatwork.com/7-principles-of-persuasion/)

And on the 2021 addition:

> **"Unity captures the power of shared identities — it's not just that we like people who are similar to us, it's that we treat them as part of us."**
> — [influenceatwork.com — The New Revision of Influence](https://www.influenceatwork.com/announcement/the-new-revision-of-influence/)

**How to apply to Hook-Story-Offer:**

- **Hook** — lead with **Scarcity** (real constraint) or **Social Proof** (one specific named customer the reader identifies with, not a logo wall).
- **Story** — the Epiphany Bridge carries **Liking** (flaws, similarity, shared pain) and **Unity** (the shared identity the reader opts into). **Authority** sits between Story and Offer — specific proof, specific engagements, specific credentials.
- **Offer** — the CTA block leans on **Reciprocity** (the free audit / discovery call *is* the gift) and **Commitment & Consistency** (the trial closes earlier in the page are the small yeses that make the booking the consistent next step).
- **Risk reversal** is Reciprocity in disguise — the project is spending its own time so the reader doesn't have to spend theirs first.

**Decision rule:** every long-form page should name *which* 2–3 Cialdini principles it's leaning on. A page that tries to use all seven is a page without focus; a page that leans on none of them is a page without pressure.

See also Cialdini's own executive summary in [HBR — *Harnessing the Science of Persuasion*](https://hbr.org/2001/10/harnessing-the-science-of-persuasion).

## Perfect Webinar — the 90-minute story-and-close template

Brunson's Perfect Webinar is the canonical structure for a long-form pitch. Roughly 90 minutes, with ~75% of sales happening in the Q&A period after the close. Out of scope for landing pages, but the *structure* is useful as a reference when drafting a webinar or a long-form sales letter.

Short version of the structure:

1. **Hook** — one sentence that earns the next 89 minutes.
2. **Big Domino slide** — the page's / webinar's thesis, stated early.
3. **Three Secrets** — each Secret breaks one False Belief (Vehicle, Internal, External), in order. Each Secret is an Epiphany Bridge story.
4. **Stack Slide** — the full offer, layered and re-stated.
5. **Price reveal** — after the stack, never before.
6. **Risk reversal** — stated explicitly, spelled out.
7. **Trial closes throughout** — not just at the end (see next section).
8. **Q&A period** — where the majority of conversions happen. Audience objections become repeated trial closes.

See [Perfect Webinar full breakdown](https://genppt.com/blog/perfect-webinar-script-russell-brunson) for the slide-by-slide structure if drafting a real webinar.

## Trial Closes and 16 Closes

**Trial closes** are micro-commitments throughout the copy, not just at the CTA. Brunson's rule: a reader who says "yes" to five small questions is far more likely to say "yes" to the big one.

Trial close patterns (use sparingly — 3–5 per long page, not one per paragraph):

- *"Does that make sense?"*
- *"If you could just [X], would that be worth it?"*
- *"Imagine [specific outcome]. Would that change your week?"*
- *"[Specific objection], right? Here's how that gets handled."*

**16 Closes** is Brunson's list of close patterns (scarcity close, price justification close, if-you-can-just-X close, etc.). Most service copy only needs 2–3 of them: risk reversal, scarcity (only if real), and the "mutual fit check" close from the Application Funnel.

For the detailed list of closes, see *Expert Secrets* directly — they're less useful out of context than most of the other frameworks in this file.

## Blair Warren — One-Sentence Persuasion

Blair Warren's 2005 essay boils influence down to 27 words. It lives alongside Brunson as one of the handful of frameworks short enough to memorise and broad enough to audit any page against:

> **"People will do anything for those who encourage their dreams, justify their failures, allay their fears, confirm their suspicions and help them throw rocks at their enemies."**
> — Blair Warren, [*The One Sentence Persuasion Course*, 2005 (PDF)](https://www.breakthroughmarketingsecrets.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/09/BlairWarren.pdf)

Note on the exact wording: the sentence has no Oxford comma before *"and help them throw rocks"*. Warren's other framing note: there is no "YOU" in the sentence. Every job is something the *reader* wants done for them — the copy that works is the copy that lets the reader feel seen, not the copy that talks at them.

**The five jobs, unpacked:**

1. **Encourage their dreams** — believe in the reader's goal, especially when the goal looks unreasonable to everyone else in the reader's life. Not cheerleading — specific belief that the reader's specific desired outcome is achievable.
2. **Justify their failures** — give the reader a scapegoat that isn't them. *"You're not failing because you're lazy; the frame you inherited was the wrong one."* Lifts shame; the reader can hear the rest of the page without defending themselves.
3. **Allay their fears** — don't dismiss the fear. Sit with it, name it, and show how the offer handles it. Dismissed fear hardens; acknowledged fear softens.
4. **Confirm their suspicions** — the thing the reader half-believes but has never heard said out loud by a professional. Confirming a suspicion is the fastest trust move in copy.
5. **Help them throw rocks at their enemies** — name the shared enemy (an approach, an industry habit, a competitor category, a mindset — never a specific person). Polarity without which the Attractive Character has no fans.

**How to apply to Hook-Story-Offer:**

- **Hook** → *confirm suspicions*. The opening sentence says the thing the reader has been quietly thinking but hasn't heard a professional say yet.
- **Backstory** → *encourage dreams* + *throw rocks at enemies*. The narrator believes in the same future the reader wants, and names the same thing the reader is tired of fighting.
- **Wall** → *justify failures*. The reason the reader has been stuck is not because of the reader; it's because the default approach was wrong.
- **Epiphany** → *allay fears*. The realisation reframes the scary part of the offer as smaller than the reader thought.
- **Offer** → *encourage dreams* + *allay fears*. The CTA restates the belief in the dream and spells out the risk reversal.

**Decision rule:** audit every draft against all five jobs. The most common failure mode is missing *justify failures* — copy that implies "you should have known better" collapses the reader's trust mid-page. If any of the five are absent, the page is persuading on fewer cylinders than it could be.

See also Warren's own site: [blairwarren.com — One Sentence Persuasion](https://www.blairwarren.com/one-sentence-persuasion/). Secondary analysis: [Breakthrough Marketing Secrets — *One Persuasive Sentence to Rule Them All*](https://www.breakthroughmarketingsecrets.com/blog/one-persuasive-sentence-to-rule-them-all-from-blair-warren/).
